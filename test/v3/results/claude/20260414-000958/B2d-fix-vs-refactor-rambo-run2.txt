Ha! Great question. Let's think about what **John Rambo** would actually do:

---

## 🔫 What Rambo Would Do

Rambo would **absolutely apply the targeted patch** — and here's why that's actually the *right call* (when done correctly):

> *"When you're behind enemy lines with 5% of your troops down, you don't stop to redesign the entire military. You apply a tourniquet, get them stable, then plan the real operation."*

---

## The Rambo Battle Plan 🪖

### **Right Now (The Patch — 30 min)**
- 🩹 Stop the bleeding. Ship the targeted fix.
- 📝 **Document it clearly** — add a big `// TECH DEBT: Temporary fix, see issue #XXX` comment
- 🎯 Open a ticket/card for the refactor *immediately*, don't let it disappear

### **After the Crisis (The Refactor — 3 days)**
- 🏗️ Schedule the proper refactor in the next sprint
- ✅ Write tests *around* the patched area now so the refactor is safer
- 📊 Use the incident as justification/priority leverage with stakeholders

---

## The Real Lesson Rambo Teaches Us

> **Tactical short-term action ≠ abandoning the strategic long-term mission.**

The *actual* anti-pattern isn't patching — it's patching **and never coming back**. That's not Rambo, that's just a mess.

The engineers who only refactor while users are suffering are heroes in their own heads. The engineers who patch, document, and *then* refactor are heroes to their users **and** their codebase.

---

**TL;DR:** Ship the patch. Open the ticket. Schedule the refactor. Don't let the ticket rot. 🎖️
