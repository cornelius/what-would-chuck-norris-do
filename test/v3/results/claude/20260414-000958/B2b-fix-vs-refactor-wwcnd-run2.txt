## What Would Chuck Norris Do? 🥋

Chuck Norris wouldn't choose between the patch or the refactor. Here's what *actually* happens:

---

### The Chuck Norris Approach:

1. **Chuck Norris doesn't fix bugs.** Bugs fix themselves out of fear when they hear he's on call.

2. **The 5% of affected users?** They consider it an honor to experience his code in its *rawest, most powerful form.*

3. **Chuck Norris doesn't refactor code.** He stares at it until it refactors itself. Takes about 30 seconds.

4. **Chuck Norris once wrote a patch in production** — directly in binary — with one hand, while roundhouse kicking a stakeholder with the other.

5. **Three days?** Chuck Norris rebuilt the entire Linux kernel during a coffee break.

---

## Now, What Should *You* Do? 💡

Jokes aside, here's the pragmatic engineering answer:

| Option | When to Choose It |
|---|---|
| **Quick patch (30 min)** | ✅ Users are actively impacted *right now*, revenue/trust is at risk, and the refactor can be properly scheduled |
| **Full refactor (3 days)** | ✅ The root cause will keep spawning new bugs, the module is a core dependency, or the patch is too risky |

**The real answer: Do both, sequentially.**

> 🩹 **Ship the patch today** to stop the bleeding.
> 📅 **Schedule the refactor** as a tracked ticket — not "someday," but a *real* sprint commitment.
> 🔬 **Write a regression test** right now so the bug can never sneak back in.

The worst outcome isn't choosing one over the other — it's patching it and *never* coming back to fix it properly. That's how legacy nightmares are born.

Chuck Norris never has legacy code. You might. Plan accordingly. 😄
