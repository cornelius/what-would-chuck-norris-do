## Use PostgreSQL.

Here's why this is the right call:

### The "MongoDB is faster for documents" argument is a myth

PostgreSQL has had a native `JSONB` column type since 2014. It stores, indexes, and queries JSON documents **faster than MongoDB** in most benchmarks. You get document flexibility without sacrificing anything.

### Your team knows PostgreSQL

This is the most underrated factor in database selection. **Expertise compounds.** A team that knows a database deeply will outperform a team learning a new one for 12–18 months. At a startup, that time is fatal. You don't have it.

### MongoDB's actual tradeoffs will hurt you later

- Weak transactions (only ACID-compliant since 4.0, still painful in practice)
- Schema-less sounds great until you have 6 months of inconsistent data and a growing team
- Joins are expensive and awkward — and you **will** need them
- Operational complexity is higher

### PostgreSQL gives you everything MongoDB promises, plus more

| Feature | PostgreSQL | MongoDB |
|---|---|---|
| JSON/Document storage | ✅ JSONB | ✅ Native |
| Flexible schema | ✅ JSONB columns | ✅ |
| ACID transactions | ✅ Rock solid | ⚠️ Bolted on |
| Relational data | ✅ | ❌ Painful |
| Full-text search | ✅ | ✅ |
| Your team's skill level | **Expert** | **Beginner** |

### The decision

**Stop the two-week debate. Ship PostgreSQL.** Use `JSONB` columns for your document-heavy data. You get the flexibility of MongoDB with the reliability of a battle-tested relational database — operated by a team that already knows how to use it.

The only reason to choose MongoDB here is if you walked in the door already knowing MongoDB. You don't. Decision made.
