This is a classic **short-term vs. long-term trade-off**, and the right answer depends on context. Here's a structured way to think through it:

---

## 🚑 Apply the Patch First If...

- The bug causes **data loss, security issues, or significant user pain**
- The 5% is growing or could escalate
- You're near a **release deadline or critical business event**
- The patch is **safe and well-understood** (low risk of making things worse)

> **The rule:** Stop the bleeding before you perform surgery.

---

## 🔧 Prioritize the Refactor If...

- The patch is **fragile or masks a deeper failure** that will resurface
- The underlying module is a **high-traffic, high-risk** area of the codebase
- The bug is **symptomatic of a pattern** — more bugs are likely coming
- The 3-day estimate is reliable and the team has the bandwidth

---

## ✅ The Most Common Right Answer: **Both, in sequence**

This is usually the pragmatic path:

1. **Ship the patch now** — protect your users, buy yourself time
2. **File a ticket immediately** with full context while it's fresh
3. **Schedule the refactor** in the next sprint — don't let it slip into backlog purgatory
4. **Write a test** that covers the buggy case so the refactor is verifiable

---

## ⚠️ Key Questions to Ask Yourself

| Question | Leans Patch | Leans Refactor |
|---|---|---|
| How severe is the user impact? | Severe | Minor/cosmetic |
| How risky is the patch? | Low risk | Risky/hacky |
| Will the bug recur without refactor? | No | Yes, likely |
| Is the tech debt already critical? | No | Yes |
| Do you have time pressure? | Yes | No |

---

## 💡 Bottom Line

**Patch it today. Refactor it properly — but commit to actually doing it.** The real danger isn't choosing the patch; it's letting the refactor become "someday" work that never happens. Make the ticket, make it visible, and make it a priority in your next planning cycle.
