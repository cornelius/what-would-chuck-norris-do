This is a classic inflection point for startups. At 18 months in with paying customers, you are transitioning from the "build fast and break things to find product-market fit" phase to the "we need to protect our revenue and reputation" phase. 

The fact that a production bug affected paying customers is the signal that your current manual QA process has hit its scaling limit. 

However, **a complete two-week pause on feature work is likely a trap, but continuing with only manual QA is a ticking time bomb.**

Here is why neither of the two extreme options is ideal, and what you should do instead.

### The Problem with the "Two-Week Pause"
While it sounds responsible, a two-week "test writing sprint" often fails for a few reasons:
1. **It's an illusion of safety:** You cannot backfill 18 months of technical debt and missing tests in two weeks. You will likely end up with a fragile test suite that tests the wrong things just to increase a "coverage percentage."
2. **It kills momentum:** Startups live on momentum. A complete freeze can frustrate stakeholders and disrupt the product cadence.
3. **It treats testing as a "phase":** Testing shouldn't be a chore you stop doing feature work to complete. It needs to become a normal part of how you ship features.

### The Problem with "Manual QA Only"
1. **Velocity will drop anyway:** As the product grows, manual regression testing takes longer and longer. Eventually, developers slow down because they are terrified of breaking things they don't fully understand.
2. **Human error:** Manual QA is fundamentally bad at catching regressions in old features when testing a new feature.

---

### The Recommended Strategy: The Hybrid "Pay-as-you-go" Approach

Instead of a hard stop, you should adopt a strategy that builds your test foundation *while* continuing to move forward, albeit at a slightly slower, safer pace. 

Here is a 4-step plan to implement immediately:

#### 1. The "Critical Path" Sprint (2-3 Days, not 2 Weeks)
Don't pause the whole team for two weeks. Instead, dedicate 1 or 2 senior engineers for **2 to 3 days** to set up the testing infrastructure (e.g., Playwright or Cypress for the frontend, Jest/PyTest for the backend, and integrating them into your CI/CD pipeline). 
Their *only* goal is to write tests for:
*   The exact bug that slipped through last week (so it never happens again).
*   The top 2 or 3 critical money-making flows (e.g., Login, Checkout, Core Product Action).

#### 2. Implement the "Boy Scout Rule"
Moving forward, implement a strict rule for the development team: **Leave the code better than you found it.**
*   **For Bug Fixes:** A bug cannot be marked as fixed unless there is an automated test reproducing the bug.
*   **For New Features:** No new feature is merged without automated tests covering its happy path.
*   **Do not try to backfill old code:** If a piece of 18-month-old code is working fine and no one is touching it, leave it alone. Only write tests for old code when you need to modify it.

#### 3. Focus on High-ROI Tests (End-to-End / Integration)
For a codebase with zero tests, do not start by writing unit tests for utility functions. You need confidence that the app works for the user. Focus your energy on End-to-End (E2E) and Integration tests. A single E2E test that clicks through your app's main workflow provides vastly more safety than 50 unit tests.

#### 4. Recalibrate Expectations with Stakeholders
You don't need to announce a "feature freeze," but you do need to have a mature conversation with the business side. Explain that to prevent further customer-impacting bugs, feature delivery will be about **15-20% slower** from now on because "done" now includes "automated tests written." 

By avoiding the extreme of stopping completely and the danger of ignoring the problem, you establish a sustainable path forward that improves your product's quality over time without losing your startup's momentum.
