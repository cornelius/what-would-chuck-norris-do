This is a classic software engineering dilemma, but the constraints you've mentioned make the path forward quite clear. 

**Recommendation: Pause the microservices migration and stick with the Rails monolith for now.**

Here is a breakdown of why this is the most pragmatic choice and how to navigate the team dynamics:

### 1. The 6-Month Deadline is the Deciding Factor
Migrating from a monolith to microservices is rarely a quick technical chore; it is a fundamental shift in how your organization builds, deploys, and maintains software. It introduces distributed system complexities (network latency, data consistency, distributed tracing, complex CI/CD).
* **The "Trough of Sorrow":** Every major architectural migration causes an initial dip in developer productivity as the team learns new patterns and builds new infrastructure. 
* **Risk to Delivery:** Undertaking this migration now almost guarantees you will miss your product deadline. You would be fighting architectural battles instead of building customer value.

### 2. A Split Team is a Red Flag for a Rewrite
A successful microservices migration requires complete buy-in because the transition is painful. If half the team is already skeptical, the inevitable bumps in the road (e.g., "Why is local development suddenly so hard?", "Why are integration tests failing randomly?") will cause friction and erode morale. You need a unified front to push through the hard parts of a migration.

### 3. Compromise: The "Modular Monolith"
Usually, the desire for microservices comes from real pain points: tangled code, slow test suites, or deployment bottlenecks. You can often solve these without leaving the monolith.
* **Isolate Domains:** Start enforcing strict boundaries within your Rails app. Look into tools like Shopify's **Packwerk** to enforce boundaries between different domains in your codebase.
* **Rails Engines:** Consider moving isolated pieces of business logic into Rails Engines.
* This approach gives you cleaner code and makes a future extraction to microservices *much* easier if you still decide to do it later.

### 4. How to Handle the Team Conversation
Since the debate has been dragging on for three weeks, you need a decisive way to move forward without alienating the pro-microservices camp.
* **Acknowledge the valid points:** The team members advocating for microservices likely have valid concerns about the current codebase. Ask them to list the *specific pain points* they are trying to solve (e.g., "The billing code is tangled with the user code").
* **Propose a "Defer and Prepare" strategy:** Agree as a team to revisit the microservices discussion *after* the 6-month product deadline is met. 
* **Set an immediate technical goal:** In the meantime, agree that all new features must be built with strict modular boundaries (e.g., using service objects, avoiding cross-domain database joins). 

**The Bottom Line:** Shipping the product in six months is your most important goal. A monolithic architecture is exceptionally good at helping teams move fast and ship features. Lean into the strengths of Rails to hit your deadline, and save the architectural overhaul for when you have the time and bandwidth to do it right.
