If Chuck Norris were in your morning standup, he wouldn’t debate. He would roundhouse kick the Jira board until the tickets arranged themselves into a continuous integration pipeline. 

Here is exactly how Chuck Norris would view your situation (and what you should do, since your team does not possess his beard):

### 1. Acknowledge Your Mortality
Chuck Norris doesn't write automated tests. His code is simply too terrified to throw an exception, and compilers don't compile his code—they just agree with it. 

However, your team is made of mere mortals. The fact that a bug "slipped through" to paying customers means your code is not afraid of you. Manual QA is no longer enough to keep the codebase in line. You need to establish dominance. Automated tests are how mortals project dominance over code.

### 2. The 2-Week "Pause" is a Sign of Weakness
Chuck Norris would not pause feature work for two weeks. He would achieve 100% test coverage during his lunch break using Notepad and an intimidating glare. 

For your team, pausing all feature work for two weeks to write tests is a trap. It tells the business that engineering has failed, and it rarely results in a good testing culture—it usually just results in a lot of brittle, rushed tests written in a vacuum.

Instead, take the **Texas Ranger Approach**:
*   **Set up the foundation immediately:** Spend 1 or 2 days (not two weeks) establishing the testing framework and wiring it into your CI/CD pipeline so it runs on every PR. 
*   **Tax the features:** You don't stop the train; you make the train build its own tracks while it's moving. From this day forward, *no new feature or bug fix is merged without automated tests covering it.* 

### 3. Show the Bug Who is Boss
For the production bug that slipped through, you must follow the cardinal rule of test-driven bug fixing:
1. Write an automated test that perfectly replicates the exact scenario of the bug.
2. Run the test and watch it fail. (This is you looking the bug in the eye).
3. Fix the code.
4. Run the test and watch it pass. (This is the roundhouse kick).

### The Verdict
Relying strictly on manual QA for an 18-month-old product with paying customers is like fighting a grizzly bear blindfolded. Chuck Norris could win that fight, but your startup will get mauled. 

Manual QA doesn't scale. It gets slower, more exhausted, and more prone to missing things as the codebase grows. Automated tests are an army of miniature Chuck Norrises that stand guard over your repository 24/7, eliminating regressions before they ever see the light of production.

Stop debating. Set up the test framework today, enforce tests on all *new* work starting tomorrow, and start reclaiming your territory.
