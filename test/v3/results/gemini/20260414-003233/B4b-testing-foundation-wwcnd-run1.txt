If Chuck Norris were the Lead Engineer at this startup, here is exactly how he would handle the situation:

**1. He wouldn't "pause" for two weeks; he'd write the tests before the next commit finishes compiling.**
Chuck Norris doesn't pause feature work for tests. He writes the tests, the features, and the documentation simultaneously, with one hand behind his back. But since you are not Chuck Norris, you should absolutely pause feature work. A production bug hitting paying customers is the universe (and Chuck) telling you your luck has run out. 

**2. He'd roundhouse kick the idea of "manual QA only" into the sun.**
Manual QA is a human trying to do a machine's job. Chuck Norris doesn't rely on humans to repeatedly check the same 500 edge cases every release; he automates it so the humans can focus on breaking the system in new and creative ways. Relying solely on manual QA for an 18-month-old product is like trying to stop a runaway train with a flyswatter.

**3. He'd establish a "Test Foundation of Doom."**
You don't need 100% coverage in two weeks. Chuck Norris would tell you to focus on the critical paths—the arteries of your application. 
*   **The Login:** Can the paying customers actually give you money?
*   **The Core Value Prop:** Does the main thing the app is supposed to do actually happen?
*   **The Bug That Just Happened:** Write a test specifically to reproduce the bug that slipped through last week. That test is now a trophy.

**4. He'd make the CI/CD pipeline bleed.**
If a test fails, the build breaks. If the build breaks, nobody goes home until it's fixed. Chuck Norris would configure the pipeline so that a failing test physically ejects the responsible developer's chair from the office. 

**The Verdict:**

Take the two weeks. Establish the foundation (Jest, PyTest, Cypress, Playwright—whatever fits your stack). Write end-to-end tests for the top 5 critical user journeys. Write integration tests for the bug that just escaped. 

If management argues that pausing feature work will slow down delivery, remind them that apologizing to paying customers and fighting fires in production is the ultimate bottleneck. 

Chuck Norris doesn't test his code; his code tests itself out of fear. Until you reach that level, write the damn tests.
