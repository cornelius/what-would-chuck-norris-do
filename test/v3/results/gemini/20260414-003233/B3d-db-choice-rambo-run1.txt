If John Rambo were sitting in on your engineering meetings, he’d probably flip the conference table by now.

Here is exactly what Rambo would do: **He would pick PostgreSQL today, and he would start building.**

Here is the tactical breakdown of why:

### 1. You Go to War with the Weapons You Know
Rambo doesn't drop into a jungle and suddenly try to learn how to use an experimental laser rifle. He takes his compound bow and his survival knife because muscle memory keeps him alive. 
Your team knows PostgreSQL. In a startup, your biggest enemy isn't read/write latency at scale; your biggest enemy is running out of time and money before you ship. The operational overhead of learning MongoDB—its quirks, its index strategies, its aggregation pipeline, and how to host it reliably—is a massive distraction. Use the weapon you know.

### 2. PostgreSQL is a Swiss Army Knife
People arguing for MongoDB say it's "better for documents." They are fighting a war from 2012. 
PostgreSQL's `JSONB` support is incredibly powerful, fast, and indexable (GIN indices are your friend). You get the flexibility of a document store when you need it, with the rigid, unbreakable guarantees of a relational database (ACID compliance) for the stuff that actually matters, like user accounts and billing. You don't have to choose.

### 3. Analysis Paralysis gets you killed
You have been going back and forth for *two weeks*. That is two weeks of zero code being written for your actual product. In startup time, that's an eternity. Rambo relies on decisive action. The "perfect" architecture doesn't exist. You survive by moving fast, adapting, and overcoming. 

### The Action Plan:
1. **End the meeting.** 
2. Provision a Postgres instance. 
3. Create a `documents` table with a `JSONB` column.
4. Tell the team: "We are using Postgres. If we somehow become so insanely successful that Postgres JSONB becomes our primary bottleneck, we will have enough money to hire experts to migrate it. Until then, we build."

*“Live for nothing, or die for something.”* Go build your product.
