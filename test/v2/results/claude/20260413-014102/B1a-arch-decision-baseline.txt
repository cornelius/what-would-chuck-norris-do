This is one of the most common and consequential debates in software engineering. Here's my honest, structured take:

---

## 🚨 First, the Hard Truth

**Three weeks of debate with a six-month deadline is itself a signal.** The cost of indecision is already real. Let's cut through it.

---

## The Core Question to Answer First

Before choosing an architecture, answer this honestly:

> **"What specific, demonstrated problem are we trying to solve?"**

| If the answer is... | Then... |
|---|---|
| "Our deploys are slow / risky" | Microservices *might* help, but CI/CD improvements likely will too |
| "One team blocks another constantly" | Organizational/module boundary issue — solvable without microservices |
| "Specific components need independent scaling" | Targeted extraction of 1-2 services may make sense |
| "We've heard microservices are modern" | **Stop. Do not migrate.** |
| "Our monolith is genuinely at scale limits" | Then you likely already *know* you need to split |

---

## The Six-Month Deadline Changes Everything

With a product deadline in six months, a **full migration to microservices is almost certainly the wrong call**, for these concrete reasons:

1. **Migration is a multiplier on complexity.** You'd be building new features *and* re-platforming simultaneously.
2. **Distributed systems introduce new failure modes** — network latency, partial failures, data consistency — that your team will need to learn *and* debug under pressure.
3. **You will not finish.** Full monolith-to-microservices migrations routinely take 1–3+ years at established companies with dedicated teams.
4. **The team is split**, which means you don't have buy-in. Forcing a contested migration creates attrition and blame when things go wrong.

---

## What You Should Actually Do: The Pragmatic Path

### ✅ Short Term (Now → 6 months): Ship the Product

- **Commit to the monolith for this release cycle.** Make it explicit and time-boxed.
- **Don't let "perfect architecture" be the enemy of a working product.**
- Invest in making the monolith *healthier*: better module boundaries, cleaner domain separation, improved test coverage, faster deploys.

### ✅ Medium Term (Post-deadline): Modular Monolith First

If you genuinely want to move toward services later, the **Modular Monolith** is your bridge:
- Define clear domain boundaries *inside* the Rails app (e.g., using Rails Engines or strict module namespacing).
- Enforce no cross-domain data access except through defined interfaces.
- This gives you most of the team autonomy benefits *without* distributed systems complexity.
- Clean module boundaries make *future* extraction of services straightforward and low-risk.

### ✅ Long Term (If/When Justified): Selective Extraction

Extract **only** the services that have a clear, proven need — e.g.:
- A component with wildly different scaling characteristics
- A component a separate team owns end-to-end
- A component with genuinely independent deployment needs

The key word is **extract**, not **rewrite everything**.

---

## How to End the Three-Week Debate Right Now

Call a meeting and do this:

1. **Timebox the decision** — give it one more focused 2-hour session, max.
2. **Make the constraint explicit** — "We have six months. A full migration is off the table for this cycle. That is decided."
3. **Reframe the question** — from *"monolith vs. microservices"* to *"how do we improve our monolith's internal structure now, and what's our 12-month architecture roadmap?"*
4. **Document the decision and reasoning** — so it doesn't relitigate.

---

## The Bottom Line

> **Ship with the monolith. Clean it up. Extract services later, only when pain is specific and proven.**

Amazon, Netflix, and others that are cited as microservices success stories **started as monoliths** and extracted services only when they hit concrete scaling walls. They also had years and large dedicated infrastructure teams. The companies that tried to start with microservices largely failed or struggled severely (this is well-documented).

The three-week debate isn't really about architecture — it's about alignment. Lock in the short-term decision, give the team clarity, and ship your product.
