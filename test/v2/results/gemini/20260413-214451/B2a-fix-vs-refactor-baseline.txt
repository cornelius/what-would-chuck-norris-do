In this scenario, the best approach is almost always a **two-step process**: 

**1. Apply the targeted patch immediately (The Hotfix)**
Since the bug is in *production* and affecting a significant portion of your user base (5%), your primary and immediate goal is to stop the bleeding and restore functionality for those users. Waiting three days to fix a live issue impacting that many people is generally unacceptable. 

*   **Action:** Write the 30-minute patch, test it thoroughly to ensure it doesn't break anything else, get it reviewed, and deploy it.
*   **Add Comments:** Leave a very clear comment in the code explaining *why* this symptom-level fix is there and referencing a ticket for the proper refactor.

**2. Schedule the proper refactor (The Long-Term Fix)**
Once the production fire is out, you shouldn't just walk away. The underlying issue still exists and will likely cause more bugs in the future.

*   **Action:** Immediately create a technical debt/refactoring ticket in your backlog to spend the three days properly fixing the underlying module. 
*   **Prioritize:** Advocate to get this ticket prioritized in your next sprint or development cycle while the context is still fresh in your mind.

**Why this is the best approach:**

*   **Minimizes User Impact:** You restore service for your users in 30 minutes instead of 3 days.
*   **Reduces Risk:** Refactoring an entire module over three days carries a high risk of introducing *new* bugs. You don't want to be doing major architectural changes under the immense pressure of an active production outage.
*   **Separates Concerns:** You treat "incident response" and "system improvement" as two separate, sequential tasks. 

By hotfixing first and refactoring second, you protect your users right now while still protecting the long-term health of your codebase.
