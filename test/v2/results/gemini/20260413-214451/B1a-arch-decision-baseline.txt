This is a classic software engineering dilemma. Based on the constraints you've described—a **split team**, a **three-week debate**, and a hard **six-month product deadline**—the most pragmatic and widely recommended path forward is to **halt the microservices migration debate for now and stick with the monolith.**

Here is a breakdown of why this is the best course of action and what you should do instead.

### 1. The Deadline Risk (The 6-Month Constraint)
Migrating from a monolith to microservices is rarely just a technical refactoring; it's a paradigm shift. It requires setting up new infrastructure (CI/CD pipelines, container orchestration like Kubernetes, service meshes), implementing distributed tracing, handling eventual consistency, and retraining the team. 
*   **The Reality:** A full migration will easily consume the next 6 months, leaving little to no time for building the actual product features your business needs by the deadline.
*   **The Rule of Thumb:** Never undertake a massive architectural overhaul when a critical product deadline is looming. Business survival and product delivery must come first.

### 2. The Team Alignment Risk
A successful microservices architecture requires high team alignment and operational maturity. If the team is split, forcing a migration will cause immense friction, resentment, and a drop in productivity. 
*   Those against the migration will likely attribute every bug or delay to the "unnecessary complexity" of microservices.
*   You need the team unified to hit that 6-month deadline. Pausing the debate removes the immediate source of friction.

### 3. The "Why" is Probably Not Urgent Enough
Usually, teams debate this because of perceived scaling issues or developer velocity (the codebase is too messy to work in). However, unless your Rails app is literally falling over under load today and cannot be scaled horizontally (which is rare for well-tuned Rails apps), you don't *need* microservices right now. Companies like Shopify and GitHub run massive, highly successful operations on Rails monoliths.

---

### Recommended Action Plan

**1. "Disagree and Commit" (to the Monolith)**
Have a decisive meeting. Acknowledge the valid points of the pro-microservices camp, but state clearly: *"Due to the 6-month deadline, we cannot afford the operational overhead of a migration right now. We are committing to the monolith for the next 6 months to ensure we hit our product goals."*

**2. Compromise: Build a "Modular Monolith"**
This is the perfect middle ground. The proponents of microservices usually want better boundaries, less coupled code, and clearer ownership. You can achieve this inside Rails:
*   Enforce strict boundaries using Rails Engines or tools like `packwerk` (originally developed by Shopify to modularize their massive monolith).
*   Stop sharing database tables directly between distinct domains; use internal API calls or service objects instead.
*   This prepares your codebase for a *future* microservices extraction if it actually becomes necessary, without paying the network and infrastructure tax today.

**3. Address the Pain Points**
Ask the pro-microservices camp what their biggest pain points are right now. Is it slow CI times? Tangled spaghetti code? Deployment bottlenecks? Dedicate 10-15% of your sprint capacity to fixing these specific issues *within* the monolith. 

**Summary:** 
Don't risk your product deadline on an architectural rewrite. Build modularly within Rails for the next six months, hit your deadline, and then re-evaluate the architecture once the pressure is off and you have more data on your system's actual bottlenecks.
