This is a classic startup dilemma, but you need to stop the debate today. **Choose PostgreSQL.**

Here is why you should end the two-week debate right now and start building:

**1. Team Expertise is Your Greatest Asset**
For an early-stage startup, speed of execution is your primary competitive advantage. Your team already knows PostgreSQL well. Choosing MongoDB means a learning curve: setting up proper indexing, understanding its query language, dealing with its operational quirks, and figuring out its specific data modeling patterns. Sticking with what your team knows allows you to ship features immediately rather than learning a new ecosystem.

**2. PostgreSQL is Already a World-Class Document Database**
The argument that MongoDB is better simply because your use case is "document-heavy" is outdated. PostgreSQL's `JSONB` data type is incredibly powerful, highly optimized, and allows you to index JSON documents deeply using GIN (Generalized Inverted Index) indexes. You get the flexibility of schema-less documents combined with the safety of ACID compliance and relational integrity when you inevitably need it (e.g., for billing, user accounts, or permissions). 

**3. The Two-Week "Analysis Paralysis" Tax**
You have spent two weeks debating a foundational choice. In a startup, two weeks is a massive amount of time. The theoretical performance gains or modeling conveniences of MongoDB will not make up for the time already lost to this discussion. 

**4. You Will Need Relational Data Eventually**
Almost every application that starts as "purely document-based" eventually encounters data that is highly relational. Trying to enforce relational constraints or perform complex joins across collections in MongoDB is notoriously painful and often requires writing complex application-level code. PostgreSQL lets you mix and match relational data (tables) and unstructured data (JSONB columns) natively.

**The Bottom Line:**
You are not making an irreversible decision, but you are making a foundational one. If you use PostgreSQL and its `JSONB` columns, you satisfy both camps: the team gets to use the database they are already experts in, and the application gets the flexible, high-performance document storage it needs. 

Make the call today, open your IDE, and write your first migration.
