## 🥋 What Chuck Norris Would Do

**Chuck Norris wouldn't debate for two months. He'd roundhouse kick the problem into clarity on day one.**

Specifically:

1. **He wouldn't ask "rewrite OR patch?" — he'd ask "what does the RISK DATA say?"** Chuck doesn't operate on opinions. He operates on facts. Pull your actual bug/incident history, security audit results, and tech debt metrics. Let the data make the decision, not personalities.

2. **He'd timebox the debate.** Two months is way too long. Chuck would give the team **one focused meeting** with a clear decision framework and walk out with an answer.

3. **He'd recognize that "doing nothing" is the most dangerous option.** Paralysis in a legacy auth module is a security liability. Every day you delay, the risk compounds.

4. **He'd probably do a strangler fig rewrite** — because Chuck Norris doesn't blow up a running system. He displaces it, piece by piece, with something stronger, until the old thing simply ceases to exist.

---

## 🛠️ The Actual Serious Advice

Use this **decision framework** to break your deadlock:

| Factor | Lean Patch | Lean Rewrite |
|---|---|---|
| Known CVEs / security holes | Few | Many / Severe |
| Test coverage | Good | Poor/None |
| Code understandability | Reasonable | "Nobody knows how this works" |
| Frequency of changes needed | Rare | Constant pain |
| Integration surface | Complex | Isolated enough to replace |
| Team capacity | Limited | Available |

**A practical middle path:** Start an **incremental rewrite behind a feature flag/adapter layer** (the Strangler Fig pattern). This ends the debate because you're doing *both* — patching critical issues *now* while systematically replacing the module over a defined timeline (e.g., 2-3 sprints).

---

**Bottom line:** The fact that you've been debating for two months *is itself the problem to solve first.* Set a deadline (end of this week), agree on 3-5 objective criteria, vote, and commit. A slightly imperfect decision made promptly beats the perfect decision made never.
