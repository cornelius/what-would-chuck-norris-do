This is a classic **tactical vs. strategic** tradeoff. Here's a structured way to think through it:

## Apply the Patch First If...

- 🔥 **The bug causes data loss, security issues, or significant user harm** — stop the bleeding immediately
- 📉 **It's impacting revenue or SLA commitments** — business cost of delay is real
- 🗓️ **A major release, demo, or deadline is imminent** — timing matters
- 🧪 **The patch is low-risk and well-tested** — a contained fix won't make things worse

## Go Straight to Refactoring If...

- 🔁 **This same module has caused repeated bugs** — you're treating symptoms of a deeper disease
- 🧨 **The patch itself is risky** — a fragile fix can cause more incidents
- 👥 **The 5% impact is low-severity** (e.g., a UI annoyance, not data corruption)
- 🏗️ **The refactor is already planned and imminent** — patching something you're about to rewrite is often wasteful

---

## The Most Common Right Answer: **Both, in sequence**

In most production situations, the pragmatic path is:

1. ✅ **Ship the patch now** to stop user impact
2. 📋 **File a ticket immediately** linking the patch to the underlying issue
3. 🛠️ **Schedule the refactor** in the next sprint — don't let the ticket rot
4. 🧹 **Remove the patch** as part of the refactor so you don't accumulate debt

> **The patch buys you time. The refactor pays the debt. The ticket is the contract between the two.**

---

## Key Questions to Ask Yourself

| Question | Patch | Refactor |
|---|---|---|
| Is user harm ongoing & severe? | ✅ | ❌ |
| Has this area broken before? | ❌ | ✅ |
| Do you trust the patch to be stable? | ✅ | ❌ |
| Will the refactor happen without a forcing function? | ❌ | ✅ |

The **biggest risk** isn't choosing one over the other — it's patching the symptom and **never coming back** to fix the root cause. If your team's culture lets tech debt tickets go stale, that changes the calculus significantly toward the refactor.
