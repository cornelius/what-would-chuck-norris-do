This is a classic debate, and the good news is: **there's a clear, reasoned way to break the tie.** Here's a structured take to help you decide:

---

## 🏆 Short Answer: **Go with PostgreSQL**

Given that your team *already knows PostgreSQL well*, the burden of proof should be on MongoDB to prove it's *worth the switching cost*. In most cases, it isn't. Here's why:

---

## 🔍 Breaking Down the "MongoDB is Faster" Argument

This claim is **often a myth** in modern contexts. Consider:

- **PostgreSQL has native JSON/JSONB support.** You can store, index, and query semi-structured documents natively. It's not just a workaround — it's a first-class feature.
- **MongoDB's speed advantage largely disappeared** after PostgreSQL 9.4+ with JSONB. Benchmarks are highly workload-dependent.
- **"Document-heavy" ≠ "needs MongoDB."** If your documents have any relational links (users → orders → products), PostgreSQL handles this *far* better without data duplication hell.
- **Premature optimization is the root of all evil.** At startup scale, developer velocity and correctness matter far more than theoretical throughput differences.

---

## ⚖️ The Real Trade-offs

| Factor | PostgreSQL | MongoDB |
|---|---|---|
| **Team familiarity** | ✅ Strong | ❌ Learning curve |
| **Document storage** | ✅ JSONB is excellent | ✅ Native |
| **Schema flexibility** | ✅ Flexible (JSONB) + enforced when needed | ✅ Schemaless |
| **Relational data** | ✅ Excellent | ⚠️ Painful |
| **ACID transactions** | ✅ Rock solid | ⚠️ Improved but complex |
| **Ecosystem/tooling** | ✅ Massive | ✅ Good |
| **Operational maturity** | ✅ Decades of battle-testing | ⚠️ More operational gotchas |
| **Horizontal scaling** | ⚠️ Harder (but rarely needed early) | ✅ Easier natively |

---

## 🚩 The Only Reasons to Pick MongoDB Instead

Be honest — do **any** of these truly apply?

1. Your data is **truly schemaless** with wildly variable, unpredictable structures that change constantly.
2. You need **massive horizontal write scaling** across many nodes *on day one* (rare for startups).
3. Your team has **strong MongoDB expertise** and zero PostgreSQL experience (the opposite of your situation).
4. You're building something like a **content management system** with deeply nested, variable documents and *no* relational needs whatsoever.

If none of these are a hard yes, PostgreSQL wins.

---

## ✅ Recommended Action Plan

1. **Choose PostgreSQL.** Stop the debate — two weeks of debate has already cost more than any DB performance difference will at your stage.
2. **Use JSONB columns** for the document-heavy parts of your schema. You get MongoDB-like flexibility *within* PostgreSQL.
3. **Define a clear trigger for re-evaluation** (e.g., "if we hit X writes/sec or Y specific bottleneck, we revisit"). This closes the loop psychologically for the MongoDB advocates.
4. **Don't over-engineer for scale you don't have yet.** Optimize for the speed of your team, not the speed of your DB.

---

## 💡 The Meta-Lesson

The best database is almost always **the one your team knows best**. Bugs, slow queries, and outages caused by an unfamiliar system will cost you far more than any theoretical performance gain. Ship faster, iterate, and let real data drive future infrastructure decisions.
