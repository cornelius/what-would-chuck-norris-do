Ha! Great question. Let's break down what the **Terminator** would actually do — and what you *should* do:

---

## 🤖 What the Terminator Would Do

The Terminator is a **mission-focused, ruthlessly pragmatic machine**. It would:

1. **Deploy the patch immediately.** The primary objective is to neutralize the threat *now*. 5% of users are suffering. Every minute counts.
2. **Log the underlying weakness.** It doesn't ignore the root cause — it **files it in memory** for a future operation.
3. **Return for the refactor.** Once the immediate mission is complete, it comes back. *"I'll be back."* (That's literally the refactor sprint.)

---

## ✅ The Actual Software Engineering Answer (same conclusion)

The Terminator's instincts are sound engineering practice here:

| Factor | Patch Now | Refactor Now |
|---|---|---|
| **User impact** | Stops bleeding immediately ✅ | Users stay broken for 3 days ❌ |
| **Risk** | Low, targeted change ✅ | Large refactor = new bugs risk ❌ |
| **Business** | Buys goodwill back fast ✅ | 3-day dev cost on live incident ❌ |
| **Tech debt** | Adds a little ⚠️ | Eliminates it ✅ |

### The right move:
1. **🚑 Ship the patch now.** Stop the bleeding.
2. **🎫 Open a tech debt ticket immediately** — with context, the root cause, and a clear rationale. Don't let it die in a backlog.
3. **📅 Schedule the refactor** in the next sprint or planning cycle with proper time and testing.

---

> **"A good engineer patches today and refactors tomorrow — a great engineer makes sure 'tomorrow' actually happens."**

The danger isn't choosing the patch. The danger is **never going back**. That's how "30-minute fixes" calcify into 5-year legacy nightmares. Don't be the machine that forgets to return. 💀
